§ I · The Problem
Complexity Classes

Two Ways of Knowing a Solution

At the heart of theoretical computer science lives a deceptively simple distinction: finding a solution versus verifying one. These two cognitive acts are formalized as complexity classes — and whether they are fundamentally equivalent is the P vs NP problem.

P Polynomial Time

Problems that a deterministic algorithm can solve in time proportional to a polynomial of the input size. Finding the shortest path between two cities. Sorting a list. Multiplying large integers.

TIME(n^k) for some constant k
NP Nondeterministic Polynomial

Problems for which a proposed solution can be verified in polynomial time, even if finding that solution may require exponential search. Sudoku. Protein folding. The Travelling Salesman.

Verifier V s.t. V(x,c) in TIME(n^k)

"If a solution can be efficiently checked, can it always be efficiently found?"

∀L ∈ NP : L ∈ P ?
Clay Mathematics Institute · Millennium Prize Problem · $1,000,000 USD · Unsolved since 1971

Most complexity theorists believe P ≠ NP — that verification is a strictly weaker cognitive act than generation. No proof exists in either direction. The question remains the central unsolved problem of mathematics and computation.
